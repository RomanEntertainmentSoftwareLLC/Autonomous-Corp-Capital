ACC V3 — Dreambot Delta Ideas
(Only new additions beyond ACC V2 and the existing roadmap)

1. Regime detector first
Before any trade logic runs, ACC should classify the market regime:
- trending up
- trending down
- sideways chop
- volatility shock
- low-liquidity / disorderly conditions

The rest of the system should adapt to the detected regime instead of using one personality in every market.

2. Strategy ensemble by regime
ACC should not rely on one “magic” strategy.
It should eventually support multiple engines and select or weight them by regime:

- Trend engine
  For strong directional continuation and breakouts

- Mean-reversion engine
  For oversold / overbought snapbacks and reversal bounces

- Relative-strength rotation engine
  For identifying the few assets showing life while the rest of the tape is weak

- Market-neutral / spread engine
  For exploiting mispricings and lag relationships when direction is messy

Goal:
No single engine should be expected to work in all conditions.

3. Ruthless risk hierarchy
Risk should have higher authority than signal generation.
Every trade and every company should operate inside hard limits such as:
- max loss per trade
- max loss per day
- max drawdown per company
- max total drawdown
- max open positions
- max correlation exposure
- max theme concentration
- forced flatten / risk-off states

ACC should always ask:
Can this trade kill the company if it is wrong?

4. Entry quality and exit quality must be separate systems
ACC should not treat exits as an afterthought.

Entry logic should focus on:
- confirmation
- context
- setup quality
- regime fit

Exit logic should focus on:
- invalidation
- take-profit behavior
- trailing protection
- time-based exits
- regime-change exits
- opportunity-cost exits

The system should be able to admit:
a decent entry can still make money with excellent exits,
and a great entry can still lose with bad exits.

5. Execution-aware decision making
Paper alpha dies if execution reality is ignored.

ACC should incorporate:
- spread
- slippage
- fee drag
- liquidity quality
- speed of move
- whether the setup still has edge after costs

A setup is not good just because price moved.
It is only good if the trade is still worth taking after friction.

6. High-quality WAIT / no-trade posture
One of the strongest behaviors ACC can learn is refusing bad trades.

WAIT should become a first-class state, not just “nothing happened.”
The system should prefer no trade when:
- confirmation is weak
- regime is hostile
- correlation is too high
- cost/friction destroys the edge
- the market is broad red mush with no exceptional setup

The dream version of ACC should be selective, not hyperactive.

7. Tradable universe vs signal-only universe
ACC should separate:
- assets that can be traded through the current broker/exchange
- assets that cannot be traded but still provide useful signal information

Example:
A non-tradable coin showing unusual strength can still act as a signal leader.
ACC should then search for tradable cousins showing the same confirmed behavior.

This prevents the system from being blind just because the broker’s tradable universe is limited.

8. Regime-specific posture switching
The dream version of ACC should not just generate BUY / SELL / HOLD.
It should switch posture by regime, such as:

- Uptrend:
  favor trend-following and relative-strength longs

- Downtrend:
  favor risk-off behavior, selective downside logic, short logic later, or cash

- Sideways:
  smaller size, quicker exits, mean-reversion only

- Volatility shock / chaos:
  reduce size sharply or stop trading entirely

This is different from raw signal generation.
It is the strategic posture of the machine.

9. Champion vs challenger strategy framework
ACC should eventually support:
- champion model / strategy = currently trusted live paper leader
- challenger model / strategy = experimental alternative being evaluated quietly

The challenger should earn promotion only through measured performance, not novelty.
This helps prevent unstable strategy swaps driven by vibes.

10. Walk-forward validation discipline
Before any major strategy or model is trusted, ACC should test it through walk-forward style evaluation:
- train on earlier data
- validate on later unseen data
- repeat across multiple market regimes

No strategy should be trusted just because it looked good on one backtest slice.

11. Post-trade attribution engine
ACC should classify why a trade won or lost.

Examples:
- good entry, bad exit
- bad entry, good recovery
- regime mismatch
- spread/slippage killed the edge
- correct idea, wrong timing
- correct timing, over-sized risk

This attribution layer should feed future rule tuning, model tuning, and agent evaluation.

12. Correlation and exposure intelligence
ACC should understand when multiple open positions are basically the same bet.
It should detect:
- correlated positions
- duplicate thematic exposure
- fake diversification
- “all roads lead to BTC” style dependence

The goal is to avoid pretending 6 altcoins equals diversification when they all move together.

13. Market-neutral and downside capability later
The dreambot eventually needs the ability to make money when the market is falling, but only after the long-side system is trustworthy.

That means later additions may include:
- downside / short logic
- inverse posture
- spread or lag exploitation
- volatility harvesting
- relative-value trades

This is a later-stage capability, not an early shortcut.

14. Strategic role tension inside the machine
ACC already has the bones of internal debate.
The dream version should lean into governed tension, where:
- ambition pushes for opportunity
- capital discipline restrains sizing
- risk veto prevents stupidity
- budget guardrails constrain AI burn
- deterministic survival logic keeps the business alive

The goal is not unanimous optimism.
The goal is governed aggression.

15. Always in the right posture, not always in the market
Final principle:
The “perfect” bot is not always trading.
It is always in the right posture for the market.

Sometimes that means:
- long
- short later
- neutral
- scaling down
- or doing nothing at all

The dream is not constant action.
The dream is persistent edge with survivability.

16. Confidence-weighted sizing
Position size should scale with setup quality, not just available cash.
Higher-confidence setups can earn more size, while weaker-but-valid setups get smaller allocations.

17. Cooldown and revenge-trade prevention
After a loss cluster, failed breakout, or volatility shock, ACC should cool itself down.
The machine should not immediately try to win everything back with more trades.

18. Session and time-of-day awareness
Some edges may be stronger or weaker depending on the time of day, liquidity window, or event schedule.
ACC should eventually learn whether specific setups are more reliable during specific trading windows.

19. Data quality scoring
Not all signals deserve equal trust.
ACC should track the quality of the data feed itself:
- stale data
- partial data
- lagging sources
- inconsistent marks
- suspicious spread anomalies

Low-trust data should automatically lower aggressiveness.

20. Edge decay detection
If a once-good setup starts underperforming, ACC should notice and reduce trust in it.
No strategy should coast forever on old glory.

21. Anti-overfitting discipline
ACC should prefer simpler, more robust edges over fragile cleverness.
If a strategy only works in one narrow slice of history, it should not earn promotion.

22. Recovery mode after drawdown
When a company or strategy falls into drawdown, ACC should shift into a more defensive operating mode:
- smaller size
- fewer entries
- higher confirmation threshold
- slower re-risking

23. Explainability snapshots
For important trades, ACC should preserve a concise explanation of why the trade was taken:
- regime
- setup type
- confidence
- risk posture
- key supporting signals

This will help debugging, attribution, and future ML labeling.

24. Portfolio construction awareness
ACC should think about the whole book, not just one coin at a time.
A good trade can still be a bad addition if it worsens overall portfolio structure.

25. Survive first, compound second
Dreambot doctrine:
The machine should optimize first for staying alive, then for compounding.
No edge matters if one bad cycle wipes out the company.
